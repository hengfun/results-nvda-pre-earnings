**Human-Readable Analysis:**

NVIDIA's China operations face a complex and rapidly evolving landscape. A significant development is China's national mandate requiring public data centers to procure over 50% of their AI chips from domestic producers, a policy that became national earlier this year. This directly shrinks NVIDIA's addressable market in a crucial segment.

Further complicating matters, there's a recent, unconfirmed report (August 22, 2025) that NVIDIA has halted production of its H20 chip, specifically designed for the Chinese market. This comes after a period of extreme volatility for the H20, which was banned in April, then unbanned in July, leading to a prior $5.5 billion write-down due to inventory issues. The rumored halt, if true, introduces significant uncertainty regarding NVIDIA's current sales in China and could lead to further inventory adjustments or a strategic pivot.

Adding another layer of complexity, the Trump administration is reportedly considering allowing sales of *newer* NVIDIA AI chips to China, albeit with a performance reduction of at least 30% and an unprecedented condition: NVIDIA and AMD would have to remit 15% of their China AI chip sales profits to the U.S. government. While this could open a pathway for more advanced chips, the profit-sharing agreement would directly impact NVIDIA's margins. It's unclear how this potential new policy aligns with the rumored H20 production halt, possibly indicating a shift to a new product line like the B30, which Bernstein estimates could generate $2.8 billion in sales in China.

In response to these pressures, Chinese domestic AI chip companies are actively forming alliances, such as the "ModChip Ecosystem Innovation Alliance," to collectively challenge NVIDIA's market dominance with "national chips and national models." This, combined with projections from Bernstein that China's AI chip localization rate will increase from 17% in 2023 to 55% by 2027, signals a strengthening competitive landscape and a long-term threat to NVIDIA's market share.

**Contradictions and Gaps:**

The most significant contradiction lies in the status of the H20 chip. While it was reportedly unbanned in July 2025, allowing sales to resume, a very recent report (August 22, 2025) suggests its production has been halted. NVIDIA has not officially commented on this. This creates a major gap in understanding NVIDIA's current sales strategy and inventory situation in China. Furthermore, the potential for the Trump administration to allow sales of *newer* (but downgraded) chips with a 15% profit share adds to the uncertainty, as it's unclear if this would supersede or run in parallel with the H20 situation or lead to a new product line like the B30.
